# In Defense of AI Mandates

Charity Majors argues that AI mandates, often criticized by engineers, can be a valuable management tool when implemented thoughtfully. Mandates provide organizational commitment, funding, and permission for temporary slowdowns, signaling that the change is a priority and that leadership accepts the trade-offs. Without a mandate, efforts to adopt AI become an unpaid burden on employees, effectively telling them it is not a priority and offering no cover from above for slipping deadlines or reduced quality. Mandates force hard conversations about timelines, enablement, and success metrics, ensuring resources are allocated and expectations are clear. However, forcing change should be a last resort, and if leaders do mandate, they must be confident they are right, as forcing employees into a wrong decision breeds resentment. Consistency is key: if AI is existential, it must be funded; if it is a nice-to-have, leaders should not later blame employees for lack of progress.

- Mandates provide organizational muscle, funding, and cover for temporary slowdowns, signaling that a change is a priority.
- Without a mandate, learning AI becomes an unpaid side project, and managers have no cover for slipping deadlines or quality.
- Mandates force leaders to define trade-offs, timelines, and success criteria, enabling honest conversations about resource allocation.
- Leaders must be consistent: if AI is existential, fund it; if it's a nice-to-have, don't later punish employees for not prioritizing it.
- Forcing a mandate should be a last resort, and if used, leaders must be correct in their direction to avoid long-term distrust.
