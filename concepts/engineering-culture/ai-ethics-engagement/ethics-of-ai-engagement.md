# Is it ethical to use AI?

Charity Majors argues that AI is simply a technology, and the ethical response is not to disengage or pursue purity, but to actively engage, learn, and shape its development and governance. She acknowledges real harms associated with AI—such as training on stolen data, labor exploitation, environmental costs, and the concentration of power—but insists that these problems are not unique to AI and that avoiding the technology is neither effective nor morally responsible. Instead, she calls for collective, disciplined work to make AI 'boring'—accountable, social, and governed responsibly. Drawing on the concept of 'against purity' from Alexis Shotwell, she emphasizes that the way to show care is to show up and build a better world, starting with open conversations at work about boundaries and better practices. The alternative, she warns, is to abandon the field to those with the fewest scruples.

- AI is just technology; its harms are not unique but require governance, not avoidance.
- Purity-driven disengagement is ineffective and narcissistic; tech workers have a moral responsibility to engage.
- Focus on collective action: make AI boring by setting boundaries, open discussions, and disciplined use.
- Start at work: acknowledge frustrations and experiment with better patterns and working agreements.
