# AI Enthusiasts vs. Skeptics: Bridging the Gap in Engineering Teams

Charity Majors argues that both AI enthusiasts and skeptics in engineering teams are responding to real existential threats: enthusiasts see discontinuous leaps in productivity (e.g., Fin/Intercom's 3x output in 9 months), while skeptics see reliability degradation, loss of institutional knowledge, and cleanup burdens from vibe-coded code. The problem is structural: wins are celebrated in public forums, while costs surface in private complaints, creating a fractured reality. Majors advocates for telling the whole story—inviting feedback on downstream consequences—and treating disagreements as engineering problems: 'What would it take for you to feel comfortable shipping code without reading it?' She emphasizes that AI amplifies existing discipline, not replaces it, and that skeptics are essential for operationalizing change safely. Sources cited include Darragh Curran's '2x' and '2x Nine Months Later' posts, Google's DORA report, and Majors' own 'Observability Engineering' 2nd edition.

- Both enthusiasts and skeptics have valid concerns: enthusiasts fear missing competitive windows, skeptics fear systems turning into unmaintainable slop.
- The asymmetry of feedback loops—wins are broadcast, costs are hidden—must be fixed by normalizing full-story communication.
- Productive conversation shifts from 'you're wrong' to 'what would it take to make you comfortable?' treating the gap as an engineering problem.
- AI amplifies existing organizational strengths and weaknesses; high discipline is a prerequisite for safe AI adoption.
