# Fragments: June 16

This collection of snippets explores the evolving impact of LLMs on software engineering culture. Dave Thomas describes how LLMs have made programming more fun by removing drudgery and speeding feedback loops. At DDD Europe, speakers emphasized that Domain-Driven Design remains crucial, and Chelsea Troy introduced the idea of managing LLM conversation registers to keep context healthy. Charity Majors warns of a growing divide between AI enthusiasts and skeptics, noting that while AI can produce discontinuous leaps in capability, it also risks degrading reliability and institutional knowledge if code is shipped without full context. Simon Willison observes that AI companies have found product-market fit with coding agents, leading to aggressive pricing. Finally, Mike Masnick reflects on the internet's failed decentralization, arguing that to avoid enshittification, tools must give users control over data and easy exit options. Together, these fragments highlight the need for balanced, disciplined, and human-centric adoption of AI in software engineering.

- LLMs are making programming more enjoyable by reducing drudgery and accelerating feedback loops.
- Domain-Driven Design remains relevant and may become more important in an LLM-augmented development process.
- Managing the 'register' of conversation with LLMs—such as starting a new context when switching modes—is key to effective use.
- AI enthusiasts and skeptics must communicate honestly about both the benefits and costs to avoid a destructive divide.
- Historical lessons from internet centralization warn that AI tools should prioritize user control and data portability to prevent enshittification.
